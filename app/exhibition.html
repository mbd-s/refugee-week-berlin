Exhibition

German version

The group exhibition The Vivid Unknown is curated by Monika Dorniak, in project room Rosalux (Wriezener Straße 12, Wedding) and other locations in Berlin. The reception is on 18 June, 19.00-23.30h. Opening hours are 19 – 24 June, 13.00-19.00h.

'The Vivid Unknown' is part of Philip Glass's music for the film Naqoyqatsi, which means 'life as war' in the Hopi language. The exhibition reflects on the world in our Anthropocene era, in which the global market has become the major economic and cultural force on the planet; control of artificial systems encounters the organic world, itself undergoing extreme transformations, leading to social disharmony, and new movements. We have transitioned to hyper-mobile interactions and interweaving in digital space, while the physical settlements of marginalised groups are constantly disturbed. Past, present and prospective natural disasters caused by climate change and war force people to leave home and make precarious escapes into the unknown.

The works use our artistic potential, and establish togetherness in times of dispersion and separation through interaction, creation, involvement and research. The main exhibition is both inside and outside project room Rosalux. In the front of the art space, there will be works by Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries, who challenges the very idea of the exhibition by distributing his art in public spaces in the city, helped by fellow activists. The social artists Elisa Dierson, Katja Marie Voigt and Felix Stumpf will develop an installation with refugee children in the project room Tiny Penthaus, in an old hotel now housing refugees, and you will be able to see some of that work in the Rosalux room. Çağlar Tahiroğlu will offer an art-therapy workshop to refugees and migrants, and the results will be on display; her work as psychologist is visible in our installation Journeys, in collaboration with refugee initiatives. Raul Walch is known for his examination of public space and activities with local communities; he will be showing a sculpture inspired by islamic architecture, made with Syrian architecture collective Xero. Matteo Valerio's textile wall installation will be activated through work with artist Monika Dorniak and performer Lingji Hon. Dorniak's sculpture refers to her ongoing process with dancers, her research in philosophy, science and her German-Polish-Lithuanian background; the work body axe (as per x,y,z) engages with inherited war trauma. Ukrainian photographer Valentyn Odnoviun re-accesses historical or socially engaged events and captures them forensically during on-site visits; his Traces of Memory looks into a former Gestapo prison in Berlin. During the artist talk, all these different approaches will be discussed further as part of the exhibition's curatorial statement.

Featured artists: Elisa Dierson, Monika Dorniak, Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries, Lingji Hon, Katja Marie Voigt, Valentyn Odnoviun, Felix Stumpf, Çağlar Tahiroğlu, Matteo Valerio, Raul Walch

Artists

Elisa Dierson, Katja Marie Voigt, Felix Stumpf

Raumkapseln

A set of equilateral triangles form three floating sculptures, that merge into one by entering a dialogue. They can be associated with deconstructed, architectural modules, that try to bust out and drift off into gravity.

These space capsules were constructed for the art space "Tiny Penthaus", in the former Hotel BelAhr, at Potsdamer Platz. This building is now the residence of refugees and a few indigenous Berliners. The hotel rooms on the 16th floor are used from artists and creatives. The children living in the hotel visit the Tiny Penthaus regularily to create more floating capsules that contribute to the childs idea that the art space is a galaxy where one can research, collect and construct.

Since 2016 Elisa Dierson, Felix Stumpf and Katja Marie Voigt have created structures in space and are researching the social factors and the efficacy of group dynamics.

Cave Community

The theory „1837 – Zum Ritornell“ by Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari is opened with a scene of a child that is afraid in the dark and finds comfort in singing a song. The song is the approach for a stabilised and becalmed centrum in the middle of chaos. It creates an existential terretorium, after Deleuze and Guattari, in which the impressions of a chaotic surrounding are forming a melodic landscape of the child.

The project Cave Community, was realised for the first time in 2016 by the artists Elisa Dierson, Katja Marie Voigt and Felix Stumpf, and applies the theories of stabilisation and existential territorium to a workshop setting. Refugee children are welcomed by children who lived in Berlin, by building caves with fabrics, cardboard, and furniture. The construction of caves as an archaic form of expression, where spontaneous architectural constructions help the group to find stability.



These individual teritories will be extended by the thoughts of the community. As a result, there will be a main square in the cave-village where regular meetings will be held. In these meetings communal and individual issues will be discussed.

For Refugee Week Berlin the artists will facilitate another workshop, and invite visitors to their cave community.

Monika Dorniak

body axe (as per x,y,z)

The sculpture body axe (as per x,y,z) is a figurative research of the human body and mind in the Anthropocene; an era that is defined by the human impact on natural processes. The sculpture's transit state is symbolyzing the paradox of the Zeitgeist: the increased understanding of nature through science and technology, and simultaneous alienation of the Self, and disembodiment. For this work the artist applies her ongoing research on the collective trauma caused by war and natural catastrophes; focusing on the long-term consequences of such suppressed and inherited memories. Deformed and turned inward the sculpture expresses the dazedness and defencelessness of a body that is plagued by memories, it may not even have experienced.




Monika Dorniak is a multi-media-artist, independent curator and researcher based in Berlin, exploring the boundaries of authorship, and a transparent, collaborative approach to forming rhizomatic connections. Her ongoing work with dancers underlines her research on the changeable body and mind in the Anthropocene. Her polish-german-lithuanian roots lead her to the theme of memories and trauma; specifically the mutual consequences of a genetic trauma on the psychology and anatomy. She connects her personal examinations with scientific research and interprets it through various media, such as performance, textile art and video works. Graduating from MA Art and Science at Central Saint Martins in 2017 with a work merging her background in fashion design, psychology, dance, she was nominated for the Studio Makers Prize by Tiffanys X Outset. She presented her work internationally, and was invited as guest lecturer at Chelsea College of Arts and Bard College Palestine. monikadorniak.com

Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries

A Paper Monument for the Paperless

A Paper Monument for the Paperless is a monument in the making, which should generate more visibility and attention to undocumented refugees. Anyone can make a contribution by requesting an A2 size poster set (in the form of a newspaper with 31 woodcut portraits of these refugees, made by a diverse group of artists and illustrators) and pasting them around or offering them to their local politicians. We are also open to suggestions for other ways to address this issue to the public and we invite people to think with us how we could generate more publicity for this initiative in the media (online, local newspaper or radio).




We worked with “We Are Here” which is a group comprising refugees living in Amsterdam. Due to neither being entitled to social housing nor allowed to work, its members are often forced to live on the street. The group decided to make visible the inhumane situation in which they live, no longer hiding, but showing the situation of refugees that are ‘out of procedure’ in The Netherlands. Thanks to the collective power of the refugees and the help of many supporters, the group has already existed for around four years. (Source: wijzijnhier.org)

Participate? Contact social artist and initiator Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries: info@himmelsbach.nl if you have an idea for how you could use the newspapers or posters, also stating which city you would like to distribute them, and adding your mailing address and the number of newspapers you would like to receive (given that we are a not-for-profit initiative, we ask for a small donation for shipping).


Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries

My socio-artistic practice has its origins in the conviction that being a studio artist is too isolated for me to translate my concerns about the world into work with social impact. I want to reach people who are outside my network and converse with people with other convictions, values and backgrounds. I want to break with my sense of social spectatorship and investigate and challenge who ‘the other’ is in our society. Social interventions that rely on co- creation and participation proved to be the best means for this. They are the key to a practice that operates at the center of society. I work on topical issues that have social and/or political urgency. Recurring issues are: citizenship and politics of exclusion, alternative histories, polarization and neoliberalism.

For these macro issues I look for translations that are close to the daily and private life of the participants and myself. The projects therefore begin by creating a personal bridge, as a tool to discuss contested, often uncomfortable questions and to create more understanding for different perspectives.

Each project is closely tailored, made for a specific context and its stakeholders. Because of the diversity of contexts and communities I use a wide range of strategies. This varies from community art projects to organizing political pranks; from making public sculptures to giving lectures and workshops; and from researching off-grid living methods to social interventions. With these projects I hope to inspire my audience to a poetic and often humorous form of engagement: a light revolt against the conventional and linear. himmelsbach.nl

Valentyn Odnoviun

Traces of Memory

[ɡəˈʃtaːpo]

External basement walls of the Berlin School of Industrial Arts and Crafts built in 1901-1905. The broad light shafts with the white glazed slotted bricks were designed to improve the light in the basement.

In 1933 the Secret State Police Office moved into the building. Walls of the Berlin School of Industrial Arts and Crafts basement became the Gestapo prison and place of unspeakable crimes. 39 cells became a place for Jewish people, opponents of regime and other objectionable “unwanted” persons.



The Gestapo had the right of preventive arrest and imprisonment without trial. “Intensified interrogation” of prisoners in the Gestapo prison could last from several hours or days to many weeks and months.

The Gestapo was responsible for implementing the so-called “final solution to the Jewish question” in Poland, the Soviet Union and to the deportation of the European Jews. After interrogations and tortures “final solution” was implemented, to be executed or being sent off to death camps...

From 1933 to 1945, approximately 15,000 persons were held in the “house prison” at the Gestapo headquarters.

The rooms for those under investigation reminds a movie theater, but the theater of films that were projected by the eyes of those awaiting another interrogation, more tortures, and death. The project is an attempt to play back those traces of memory.

During the last few years researches has been mostly linked with historical or socially engaged events and problems through the “abstract”-looking image, working with the imagination of the viewer to create more concrete communication with the help of interpretation and conceptual thinking.

Çağlar Tahiroğlu

Journeys

For my ongoing participatory photography research, Journeys, I have collaborated with organisations working with refugees and migrants. The exiled participants were asked about what they have brought with them from their homeland. This can be an object, a story… etc. After the interview process where the participants tell their journey, we decide together how to photograph the object that they have brought with them. The process was considered as reflective even a symbolic way of anchoring them in their new surrenderings, going through bereavement, loss as well as, a way of building and new hopes.

Workshop

My workshop is an art-therapy inspired free creation space. Participants are invited to imagine their homeland: it may be where they come from, or where they feel belonging. From that imagination, they are encouraged to create with simple art materials: creativity is given more importance then aesthetics. The creation process is imagined as mental journey where we reflect on our sense of belonging and symbolic borders. We hang the pictures to a board or to the wall while the workshop, creating an instant exhibition.



Çağlar Tahiroğlu is a Turkish born, French trained, interdisciplinary practitioner of psychology and art. She qualified as a clinical psychologist in 2011. From December 2015, she worked in Calais and at the Grande-Synthe refugee ‘jungle’ camp as a volunteer. She subsequently became a member of Médecins Sans Frontières (Doctors Without Borders) team as a mental health activity manager, working in refugee camps in Europe and in Africa. Aside from her humanitarian work, she is currently studying on the MA Art and Science course at Central Saint Martins to discover the synergy between her different practices. Her current research centres on the themes of creativity, exile and women’s experiences. Taking her field work in the refugee camps of Northern France and Africa as a point of departure, Çağlar will talk about some aspects of the current situation concerning humanitarian issues and how creative, non-binary thinking may help to re-consider our perspectives of art practice and care systems. cargocollective.com/caglartahiroglu

Matteo Valerio

Dream Of Intangible Cultures

The work Dream Of Intangible Cultures is part of the project New Travels For Marco Polo, a research on materials and processes that could be updated even though traditional, representing an approach to production that could harmonize work, daily life and nature. The textiles dyed with plants serve as a landscape for the prints, informed by symbols or stories of businesses, or club events, brought by foreigners in an hosting country contributing in renovating its economic and cultural identity. The canvas made specifically for one of the walls of the space becomes another backdrop, a trompe l’oeil containing fragments of our native history of migration and settlement. A stratum that could be removed, tested, changed, folded, performed increasing its relations and content, separating while preserving itself. This is the first leg for this work that will incorporate other functions in other places, until consumed.

Ausstellung

English version

Die Gruppenausstellung The Vivid Unknown ist kuratiert von Monika Dorniak, und findet im Projektraum rosalux, und unterschiedlichen Orten in Berlin statt. Die Eröffnung ist am 18.06.2018 (19.00-23.30h). Öffnungszeiten: 19.-24.06.2018 (13.00-19.00h).

The Vivid Unknown ist ein Stück von Philip Glass's Musik für den Film Naqoyqatsi, was in der Hopi Sprache 'life as war' bedeutet. Die Ausstellung beschäftigt sich mit der Welt in der Ära des Anthropozän, in welcher der globale Markt als wichtigster kultureller und wirtschaftlicher Einflussfaktor agiert. Die Kontrolle von artifiziellen System trifft auf die organische Welt, welche selbst in einem extremen Wandel ist. Diese Differenzen führen zu sozialen Reibungen, und neuen Bewegungen im digitalen, sowie analogen Raum. Durch die Verwebungen mit dem digitalen Raum haben sich interkulturelle Verbindungen stabilisiert, wobei die physischen Niederlassungen von marginalisierten Gruppen kontinuierlicher Gefahr ausgesetzt sind. Vergangene, aktuelle und zukünftige durch Klimawandel hervorgerufene Naturkatastrophen, sowie Kriege zwingen Menschen dazu ihr zu Hause zu verlassen, und prekäre Fluchten in das Unbekannte zu unternehmen.

Die Ausstellung zeigt Arbeiten, in der die Anwesenheit des Künstlers als Katalysator für Kreativität und Mobilisierung in der Gesellschaft agiert. Durch interaktive, partizipatorische und analytische Methoden vermitteln die Künstler die Zusammengehörigkeit in Zeiten von Separation und Zerstreuung. Die Ausstellung ist gleichzeitig innerhalb und außerhalb des Projektraumes rosalux. Der niederländische Künstler Domenique Himmelsbach de Vries nutzt die Fassade des Gebäudes für seine Arbeiten, und stellt das traditionelle Konzept einer Ausstellung in Frage, indem er seine Arbeit mit weiteren Aktivisten an öffentlichen Orten anbringen wird. Die sozialen Künstler Elisa Dierson, Katja Marie Voigt und Felix Stumpf entwickeln eine Installation mit Flüchtlingskindern in dem Projektraum TIny Penthaus, ein ehemaliges Hotel, welches nun Wohnort für Flüchtlinge ist. Çağlar Tahiroğlu bietet Flüchtlingen und Migranten einen Kunsttherapie Workshop an, welcher mit einer temporären Ausstellung in der Ausstellung abschließt. Ihre Arbeit als Psychologin ist in ihrer Arbeit Journeys erkennbar, welche eine Kollaboration mit Flüchtlingsinitiativen ist. Raul Walch ist bekannt für seine kritische Auseinandersetzung mit dem öffentlichen Raum, und der Interaktion mit lokalen Gemeinschaften; die präsente Skulptur ist inspiriert von islamischer Architektur, und ist eine Kollaboration mit dem Syrischen Architekturkollektiv Xero. Matteo Valerio's textile Installation wird durch den Austausch mit Kuratorin Monika Dorniak, und Performerin Lingji Hon aktiviert. Dorniak's Skulptur bezieht sich auf ihre kontinuierliche Arbeit mit Tänzern, und ihre Forschung in Philosophie, Wissenschaft und ihrem Deutsch-Polnisch-Litauischen Hintergrund; die Arbeit body axe (as per x,y,z) beschäftigt sich mit vererbten Kriegstraumata. Der ukrainische Fotograf Valentyn Odnoviun ruft historisches und soziale Geschehnisse wieder auf, und hält diese durch seine Besuche vor Ort forensisch fest; seine Arbeit Traces of Memory betrachtet die ehemaligen Gestapo Gefängnisse in Berlin. In den Künstlergesprächen werden die verschiedenen Denkansätze als Teil des kuratorischen Konzepts aktiv miteinander in Verbindung gebracht.

Raumkapseln

Modulare Elemente in Form von gleichseitigen Dreiecken bilden, kompositorisch zusammengefügt, drei Skulpturen. Schwebend im Raum gehen sie einen Dialog untereinander ein. Sie erinnern an dekonstruierte architektonische Module. Diese brechen aus, driften ab – in die Schwerelosigkeit.

Diese "Raumkapseln" wurden für den Kunstraum "Tiny Penthaus" geschaffen. Er befindet sich im ehemaligen Hotel BelAhr am Potsdamer Platz. Dort leben Geflüchtete und vereinzelt alt-Berliner Mieter*innen aus Vorwendezeiten. Die Hotelzimmer der Etage 16 des Hochhauses wurden von Künstler*innen und Kulturschaffenden bezogen.

Die Kinder aus dem Hotel besuchen wöchentlich das Tiny Penthaus, um weitere eigene Kapseln beizusteuern. Kapseln, da in ihrer Fantasie der Raum eine "Galaxie“ ist. Ein "Space" des Sammelns, Schaffens und Konstruierens – in ihr schweben die Raumkapseln.

Elisa Dierson, Felix Stumpf und Katja Marie Voigt entwickeln seit 2016 zusammen raumbildende Strukturen und untersuchen deren soziale Eigenschaften und gruppendynamische Wirkungskräfte.

Cave Community

Die Theorie „1837 – Zum Ritornell“ von Gilles Deleuze und Felix Guattari wird von der Szene eines gehenden Kindeseröffnet, das „im Dunkeln Angst bekommt“ und sich beruhigt, indem es ein Liedchen singt. Das Lied wird „Ansatz […] für ein stabilisierendes und beruhigendes Zentrum mitten im Chaos.“ Es erzeugt für das Kind – in den Begriffen Deleuzes und Guattaris – ein Existenzterritorium, es gliedert die Eindrücke einer chaotischen Umgebung zu einer Landschaft: der Melodielandschaft des Kindes.

Das Projekt Cave Community, erstmals im Jahr 2016 von den Künstler*innen Elisa Dierson, Katja Marie Voigt und Felix Stumpf realisiert, überträgt diesen Gedanken der „Stabilisierung“ und des "Existenzterritoriums" auf eine konkrete künstlerische Anwendung. Aus Stoff, Pappen, Kartonagen und diversen Möbeln bauen die Neu-Berliner Kinder zusammen mit Alt-Berliner Kindern über mehrere Tage zusammen Höhlen. Der Bau von Höhlen als archaische Ausdrucksform – Spontane architektonische Gebilde werden zum Stabilitätszentrum für Kinder.



Erweitert werden diese individuellen Territorien, die Höhlen, durch den Gedanken einer Gemeinschaft. So entsteht im Mittelpunkt des Höhlendorfs eine Art Dorfplatz. Hier finden regelmäßig Treffen statt, bei dem gemeinschaftliche und individuelle Belange verhandelt werden.

Monika Dorniak

Body Axe (as per X/Y/Z)

Die Skulptur Body Axe (as per x,y,z) ist eine figürliche Untersuchung des Menschen in einer anthropozentrischen Ära, in der die Natur durch die Prozesse des Menschen beeinflusst wird. Der menschliche Körper und Geist wird hier in einem Zwischenzustand präsentiert, der eine Gegenüberstellung aktueller Paradigmen symbolisiert: das komplexe wissenschaftliche Verständnis wird hierbei überschattet von der verlorenen Verbindung des verfremdeten Bewusstseins zu der physischen und mentalen Materie. In dieser Arbeit beschäftigt sich die Künstlerin speziell mit dem kollektiven, unverarbeiteten Trauma das durch Kriegszustände ausgelöst wurde, und dessen langzeitliche Auswirkungen durch die Gesellschaft, sowie Politik unterdrückt werden. In sich gekehrt und deformiert, erkennt man in der Skulptur die Verstörtheit und Hilflosigkeit eines von fremden Erinnerungen geplagten Körpers.

Valentyn Odnoviun

Traces of Memory

[ɡəˈʃtaːpo]

Kellerwände der Berliner Schule für Gewerbe und Handwerk, erbaut zwischen 1901-1905. Die breit angelegten Lichtschächte mit weiß glänzenden Ziegelsteinen wurden entwickelt um die Lichtsituation im Keller zu verbessern.

Im Jahr 1933 zog die Geheime Staatspolizei in das Gebäude ein. Die Wände der Berliner Schule für Gewerbe und Handwerk wurden fortan das Gefängnis der Gestapo, einem Ort von entsetzlichen Verbrechen. In 39 Zellen wurden Juden, Gegner des Systems, oder andere ‘unerwünschte’ Personen gefangen genommen.



Die Gestapo hatte das Recht präventive Festnahmen und Gefängnishaft ohne Strafverfahren durchzuführen. Intensive Verhörungen von den Gefangenen der Gestapo konnten mehrere Stunden, bis hinzu Tagen, Wochen oder Monaten dauern.

Die Gestapo war verantwortlich für die Ausführung der so-genannten ‘Judenfrage’ in Polen, der Sowietunion oder der Deportation von Europäischen Juden. Nach Verhörungen und Torturen wurde sich für Hinrichtung oder der Deportation entschieden.

Zwischen 1933 und 1945 wurden ungefähr 15000 Personen in den Gefängnissen der Hauptverwaltung der Gestapo festgehalten.

Die Räume für die Investigationen erinnern an ein Kino, aber eines in dem die Filme von Augen projiziert wurden die weitere Verhöre, Folter, oder den Tod erwarten würden. Dieses Projekt ist ein Versuch die Spuren der Erinnerung wieder aufzurufen.
